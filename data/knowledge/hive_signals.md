# Hive Signals

## Purpose

This note gives cautious guidance for reading simple hive signals, whether from
manual observation or from sample edge-signal style inputs (such as basic
temperature, humidity, and activity notes). It is field triage and reasoning
support only. Signals are not a diagnosis. Always confirm by physical
inspection. HyveGrid is not a certified disease diagnosis tool.

## Field use

Use this note when a signal or observation seems off: temperature rising,
humidity dropping, activity falling, bees clustering outside, water scarcity, or
poor ventilation. It is a checklist for what these patterns might mean and what
to verify before acting.

## Key checks

- **Rising temperature:** A rising internal temperature can mean heat stress,
  poor ventilation, crowding, or (rarely) a serious problem. It is not, by
  itself, a diagnosis.
- **Dropping humidity:** Very dry air can point to heat, wind, or water stress
  for the colony. Low humidity alone is not conclusive.
- **Low activity:** Low entrance activity may mean a weak colony, a queen
  problem, hunger, swarming, weather, or stress. It needs physical checking.
- **Clustering outside (bearding):** Bees clustering outside the entrance in hot
  afternoons can be normal bearding for cooling. It can also mean crowding, poor
  ventilation, or heat stress. Context matters.
- **Water scarcity:** Lack of clean nearby water stresses bees, especially in
  heat. Check the water source, not just the hive.
- **Ventilation and shade:** Poor airflow and too much direct sun raise heat and
  stress. Check shade, entrance clearance, and airflow around the hive.

## Possible concern

Possible concern signs include: a sustained temperature rise with no cooling,
humidity dropping and staying low, activity falling over several days, constant
clustering outside even when cool, no available clean water, or a hive in full
afternoon sun with poor airflow. Any one signal alone is weak; a combination is
more worrying.

## Check first

- Confirm by physical inspection before drawing conclusions.
- Check shade, direct sun exposure, and airflow around the hive.
- Check the water source for availability and cleanliness.
- Check whether clustering is happening in cool weather too (more concerning).
- Check entrance blockage, colony strength, brood, and food stores if safe to
  open the hive.
- Compare today's signals against the last several days, not just one reading.

## Avoid doing immediately

- Avoid treating a single signal as a confirmed problem.
- Avoid sealing the hive or reducing ventilation to "fix" heat stress.
- Avoid spraying water or chemicals into the hive.
- Avoid opening the hive repeatedly or in poor weather.
- Avoid assuming disease from temperature or humidity alone.

## Suggested next step

Add shade and clean nearby water if heat or water stress is likely, improve
airflow without sealing the hive, and record signals over several days to see the
trend. If a cluster of signals points to a real problem, plan a careful physical
inspection.

## When to escalate

Consult an experienced beekeeper or extension officer when: signals stay
abnormal across several days and a physical inspection also looks wrong, the
colony is clearly declining, or you cannot tell whether the pattern is normal
seasonal behavior or a real problem. HyveGrid provides field triage and next-step
guidance only; signals are not a diagnosis without physical inspection.
