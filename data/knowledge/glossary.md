# Glossary

## Purpose

This note defines common apiculture terms used across the HyveGrid knowledge
notes, in plain language. It is for beekeepers and extension workers using the
offline field assistant. Definitions here are simplified and cautious, not a
substitute for formal training. Consult an experienced beekeeper or extension
officer when unsure.

## Field use

Use this glossary as a quick reference while reading the other notes or during a
field check. Terms are kept short and practical.

## Key checks

- **Apiary:** A place where one or more beehives are kept; a bee yard.
- **Brood:** The developing bees inside the hive: eggs, larvae, and pupae. A
  healthy brood pattern is usually solid and even.
- **Capped honey:** Honey that bees have dried enough and sealed with a wax cap.
  Mostly capped frames are the cautious target for harvest.
- **Uncapped nectar:** Runny, not-yet-dried nectar in open cells. It holds more
  water and can ferment, so it is higher risk for harvest.
- **Forage:** The flowers, plants, trees, and crops that bees collect nectar and
  pollen from. Forage diversity helps keep colonies strong.
- **Nectar:** The sugary liquid bees collect from flowers and turn into honey.
- **Pollen:** The protein-rich powder bees collect from flowers and use to feed
  brood. Pollen stores are part of colony health.
- **Queen signs:** Indirect evidence that a queen is present and laying, such as
  eggs and a steady brood pattern. Seeing eggs is usually the most reliable sign.
- **Hive stand:** The structure that holds the hive off the ground. A stable,
  raised stand reduces damp and some pest access.
- **Bearding / clustering outside:** Bees gathering at the entrance or outside
  the hive, often in hot afternoons. It can be normal cooling behavior or a sign
  of heat stress, crowding, or poor ventilation.
- **Pest pressure:** The level of trouble from ants, wax moths, small hive
  beetles, and other pests. Higher pressure weakens a colony.
- **Moisture risk:** The chance that wet honey or damp conditions cause
  fermentation, mold, or rot. Higher when too much uncapped nectar is harvested.
- **Pesticide drift:** Movement of sprayed chemicals beyond the target area,
  which can poison foraging bees. A major real risk near treated farms.
- **Flowering gap:** A period when little or nothing is blooming (a dearth),
  when colonies can run short of food if stores are low.
- **Extension worker:** A trained person (often government or project based) who
  advises farmers and beekeepers on good practice and local rules.

## Possible concern

Use caution when any term here is applied to a real hive: a single sign (such as
bearding or low activity) is rarely enough to reach a conclusion. Possible
concerns should always be confirmed by physical inspection.

## Check first

Confirm meaning and context against what you actually see in the field before
acting on a term. When a note says "check first," confirm by physical inspection.

## Avoid doing immediately

Avoid acting on a definition or a single observed sign as if it were a confirmed
diagnosis. Avoid spraying chemicals into or near the hive to "fix" a sign.

## Suggested next step

Keep this glossary handy while reading the other notes. If a term or a field sign
is unclear, record what you saw and re-inspect rather than guessing.

## When to escalate

Consult an experienced beekeeper or extension officer when a term describes
something you cannot interpret confidently, or when field signs keep appearing
that you do not understand.
