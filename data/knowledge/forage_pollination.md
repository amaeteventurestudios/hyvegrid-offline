# Forage and Pollination

## Purpose

This note gives cautious guidance on forage diversity, flowering seasons, and
pollination for bee colonies, with African and Nigerian field context in mind.
It is field triage and reasoning support only. HyveGrid is not a certified
diagnostic tool. Confirm conditions on the ground and consult an experienced
beekeeper or extension officer when needed.

## Field use

Use this note when choosing a site, planning hive numbers, or understanding why
colonies are strong or weak at different times of year. It is a checklist for
thinking about what the bees are feeding on and what the surrounding land offers.

## Key checks

- **Forage diversity:** Bees do best with many different flowering plants, trees,
  and crops rather than a single source. Diverse forage spreads risk across the
  season.
- **Flowering calendars:** Notice which plants flower and when. A rough local
  flowering calendar helps predict good and bad periods.
- **Seasonal forage gaps:** Most areas have a dearth (a flowering gap) when
  little is blooming. Colonies can starve or weaken during these gaps if stores
  are low.
- **Crop dependence risk:** Relying heavily on one crop is risky. When that crop
  finishes flowering or is sprayed, the colony loses its main food source at
  once.
- **Pesticide coordination during flowering:** Flowering crops are exactly when
  bees are most exposed. Coordinate spray timing with farmers and avoid spraying
  while flowers are open and bees are flying.
- **Pollination value and limits:** Bees pollinate many crops, which can raise
  yields. But pollination benefit depends on crop type, timing, colony strength,
  and weather. Do not overpromise guaranteed yield gains.
- **Competition from other colonies:** Too many colonies in one area compete for
  limited flowers. This weakens all colonies and lowers harvests.
- **African / Nigerian examples:** Useful forage sources include mango flowering,
  pepper and vegetable flowers, cassava surroundings (nearby weeds and fallow
  flowers, rather than cassava itself), and wild flowering plants, shrubs, and
  trees. Cassava should not be assumed to provide enough forage on its own.

## Possible concern

Possible concern signs include: only one main flowering source with nothing
else, a known flowering gap with low stores, heavy spraying on flowering crops
without coordination, many colonies crowded into limited forage, or colonies
losing weight and brood after a crop finishes flowering.

## Check first

- Confirm by physical inspection: walk the area and note what is flowering.
- Check how much diversity exists beyond the main crop.
- Ask farmers what they spray and when, especially during flowering.
- Check colony stores before and after the expected flowering gap.
- Count existing colonies nearby to estimate competition.
- Note whether mango, vegetables, pepper, and wild plants overlap in time or
  leave a gap.

## Avoid doing immediately

- Avoid placing a large number of colonies on an untested forage base.
- Avoid assuming cassava surroundings alone will feed the bees.
- Avoid relying on a single crop as the only food source.
- Avoid spraying or allowing spraying on flowering crops while bees are flying.
- Avoid expanding hive numbers without monitoring colony strength first.

## Suggested next step

Build a simple local flowering calendar (month by month, main sources), match
hive numbers to the weakest part of the year, and plan feeding or reduced hive
numbers for known gaps. Keep notes on which sources the bees actually use.

## When to escalate

Consult an experienced beekeeper or extension officer when: forage is clearly
insufficient, colonies decline after a crop finishes, spraying on flowering
crops cannot be coordinated, or you are unsure how many colonies the area can
support. HyveGrid provides field triage and planning support only.
