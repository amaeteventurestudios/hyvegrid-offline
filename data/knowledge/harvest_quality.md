# Harvest Quality

## Purpose

This note gives cautious guidance for harvesting and handling honey so that it is
safe, stable, and traceable. It is field triage and reasoning support only.
HyveGrid is not a certified food-safety or disease diagnosis tool. Follow local
food-handling rules and consult an experienced beekeeper or extension officer
when needed.

## Field use

Use this note before and during a honey harvest. It is a checklist for what to
take, what to leave, how to keep equipment clean, and how to avoid contaminating
or spoiling the honey.

## Key checks

- **Capped honey vs uncapped nectar:** Harvest frames that are mostly capped
  (sealed with wax). Uncapped, runny nectar holds more water and can ferment.
  As a cautious field rule, wait until a frame is mostly capped before taking it.
- **Moisture and fermentation risk:** Wetter honey (lots of uncapped cells) can
  ferment and spoil. Avoid mixing wet uncapped nectar into a batch of finished
  honey.
- **Do not harvest brood frames:** Never cut or extract frames that contain
  brood. Brood belongs to the colony and should stay in the hive.
- **Leave enough stores:** Do not strip the colony. Leave enough honey and pollen
  for the bees to survive the dearth period. Over-harvesting weakens or kills
  colonies.
- **Clean, food-safe equipment:** Use clean, food-grade containers and tools.
  Dirty or rusty equipment contaminates honey and shortens its shelf life.
- **Contamination sources:** Watch for smoke residue, dirt, dust, chemical fumes,
  fuel, and heat. Honey absorbs smells and flavors easily.
- **Filtering and storage basics:** Remove wax bits and debris with a clean
  filter or strainer. Store in sealed, food-grade containers away from heat,
  sun, and strong smells.
- **Traceability basics:** Keep simple records: date, hive or apiary, location,
  and approximate amount. Basic records help with quality review and any later
  recall or buyer questions.

## Possible concern

Possible concern signs include: harvesting mostly uncapped, runny nectar; mixing
wet nectar into finished honey; cutting brood comb; taking too much and leaving
the colony short of food; using dirty, rusty, or chemical-tainted containers;
exposing honey to smoke, dirt, or heat; or storing it open in warm, smelly
places.

## Check first

- Confirm by physical inspection that frames are mostly capped before harvesting.
- Check that each frame is honey, not brood.
- Check that containers and tools are clean, dry, and food-grade.
- Check the work area for smoke, dirt, fuel, and chemical exposure.
- Estimate how much to leave behind so the colony has food through the dearth.
- Check that the storage spot is cool, dry, sealed, and free of strong smells.

## Avoid doing immediately

- Avoid harvesting frames that are mostly uncapped or runny.
- Avoid harvesting or damaging brood frames.
- Avoid emptying the hive and leaving the colony with no stores.
- Avoid using dirty, rusty, or non-food containers.
- Avoid exposing honey to direct heat, smoke, or chemical fumes.
- Avoid storing honey open or in warm, smelly places.

## Suggested next step

After harvest, label each container (date, apiary/hive, amount), filter out wax
and debris if not already done, and store sealed in a cool, dry place. Record
what was taken and what was left for the bees. Plan to check the colony's
remaining stores in the following days.

## When to escalate

Consult an experienced beekeeper or extension officer when: you are unsure
whether honey is dry enough to harvest, the colony looks weak before harvest, you
suspect contamination, or you need guidance on local food-handling and labeling
rules. HyveGrid provides field triage and next-step guidance only; it does not
certify food safety.
