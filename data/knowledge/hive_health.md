# Hive Health

## Purpose

This note gives cautious field guidance for checking the general health and
strength of a bee colony. It supports field triage and reasoning only.
HyveGrid is not a certified disease diagnosis tool. Confirm any real concern by
physical inspection and consult an experienced beekeeper or extension officer
when needed.

## Field use

Use this note during routine hive checks when something looks unusual: low
activity at the entrance, a colony that seems weak, uneven brood, missing queen
signs, low food stores, odd smell, or signs of ants, wax moth, or beetles. It is
a checklist to slow down and look carefully before acting.

## Key checks

- **Hive activity:** Watch the entrance during a warm part of the day. Low or no
  activity may mean a weak colony, a queen problem, swarming, hunger, or stress.
  High activity alone is not proof of health.
- **Colony strength:** Estimate adult bee numbers across frames. A sparse,
  shrinking population is a weak-colony warning sign.
- **Queen signs:** Look for eggs (tiny, standing on end in cells), young larvae,
  and a steady, even brood pattern. Seeing the queen herself is good but not
  required; eggs are the most reliable sign that she was present recently.
- **Brood pattern:** Healthy brood is concentrated and solid in a rough oval.
  Patchy, spotty, or scattered brood may point to queen, disease, or stress
  issues.
- **Eggs / larvae / brood:** Confirm eggs are present. Larvae should look pearly
  white and moist, not dry, discolored, or sunken.
- **Food stores:** Check for stored nectar/honey and pollen. Low stores,
  especially in a dearth, can weaken or kill a colony.
- **Smell:** A healthy hive has a mild, warm, sweet smell. A sour, rotten, or
  fishy smell is a possible concern and warrants closer inspection.

## Possible concern

Possible concern signs include: very low entrance activity, a shrinking or
sparse population, no eggs or young brood, patchy brood, discolored or sunken
larvae, low food stores, a sour or rotten smell, ants entering the hive, wax
moth webbing or tunnels, small hive beetles or their slime, or a sudden change
in behavior.

## Check first

- Confirm by physical inspection before drawing conclusions.
- Check whether the entrance is blocked by bees, debris, or pests.
- Check for eggs and a solid brood pattern across several frames.
- Check food stores and pollen.
- Check whether ants are only near the stand or actually entering the hive.
- Look for wax moth webbing, tunnels, or larvae in unused comb.
- Look for small hive beetles hiding in corners or under the floor.
- Check recent disturbance, weather, spray, or feeding changes.

## Avoid doing immediately

- Avoid harvesting honey from a weak or stressed colony.
- Avoid splitting, moving, or merging the colony before checking its condition.
- Avoid spraying chemicals into or near the hive.
- Avoid opening the hive repeatedly or for long periods in poor weather.
- Avoid assuming a disease from smell or activity alone.

## Suggested next step

Record what you observed (date, hive, activity, queen signs, brood pattern, food
stores, smell, pests). If the colony looks weak or queenless, provide support
such as feeding if stores are low, reduce entrance size if under pest pressure,
and monitor closely over the next several days. Plan a re-inspection rather than
a one-time judgment.

## When to escalate

Consult an experienced beekeeper or extension officer when: there are no eggs
and no queen signs over repeated checks, brood looks diseased (discolored, dry,
sunken, or foul-smelling), the colony keeps declining, wax moth or beetle
pressure is heavy, or you are unsure how to interpret what you see. HyveGrid
provides field triage and next-step guidance only; it does not certify disease
diagnosis.
